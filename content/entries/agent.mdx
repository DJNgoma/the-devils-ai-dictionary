  A software system granted just enough initiative to impress a conference panel and just enough unpredictability to alarm operations.
plainDefinition: |
  In current AI practice, an agent is usually a system that can pursue a goal across multiple steps by planning, using tools, consulting memory or retrieval, and adjusting its actions based on intermediate results.
whyExists: |
  The term exists because some useful tasks are not one-shot question answering. They require sequences: inspect, decide, act, check, retry, stop. "Agent" is the label for packaging those loops into something more autonomous than a single prompt-response exchange.
misuse: |
  It gets abused whenever a chatbot can do more than one thing. A model with a button labelled "send email" is not automatically an agent. Sometimes the word is doing no more than replacing "workflow" because the latter sounds unromantic.
practicalMeaning: |
  In practice it usually means orchestration, tool permissions, planning heuristics, state management, timeouts, approvals, and a carefully chosen boundary around what the system is allowed to decide alone.
example: |
  "The support agent was useful not because it had a heroic inner life, but because it could look up the account, draft the response, and ask for approval before sending it."

Useful agents exist. They just tend to look less like science fiction and more like tightly scoped systems with clear permissions, strong fallbacks, and very little romance.
