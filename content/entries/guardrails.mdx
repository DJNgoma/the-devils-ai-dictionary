devilDefinition: |
  The collection of fences, checks, filters, and sternly worded hopes designed to stop a model from wandering into places the product team cannot afford.
plainDefinition: |
  Guardrails are mechanisms used to constrain or monitor model behaviour, such as policy filters, tool permissions, schema validation, prompt rules, moderation systems, human approvals, or output checks.
whyExists: |
  Models are flexible in exactly the ways that make product owners nervous. Guardrails exist to narrow behaviour into something safe, compliant, or at least operationally manageable.
misuse: |
  The term gets invoked as though one moderation layer solves the entire problem. Real guardrails are layered, task-specific, and never total. A vague promise of guardrails often means someone has remembered risk late in the process and is trying to sound calmer than they feel.
practicalMeaning: |
  In practice it usually means a stack of constraints before, during, and after the model call: restricted tools, validation logic, refusal policies, monitoring, and human review for higher-risk actions. The strongest guardrail is often reducing what the system is allowed to do in the first place.
example: |
  "The useful guardrail was not the disclaimer. It was the rule that stopped the assistant from sending anything externally without a human sign-off."
