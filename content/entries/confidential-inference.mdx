plainDefinition: |
  Confidential inference is inference designed so prompts, retrieved context, outputs, and sometimes model weights are protected from unauthorised visibility while the model runs, often through confidential computing, tenant isolation, encryption, access controls, and audit logs.
whyExists: |
  It exists because sensitive AI workloads cannot be judged only by whether the vendor promises not to train on the data. Regulated, proprietary, personal, and legally privileged inputs need a clearer account of who can see what during execution.
misuse: |
  It is misused when one protected layer is treated as total secrecy. Encrypted transport, a private endpoint, or a trusted execution environment does not automatically solve logging, telemetry, retrieval leakage, administrator access, key custody, output handling, or side-channel risk.
practicalMeaning: |
  In practice it means writing down the threat model, mapping which parties can see prompts and outputs, checking what lands in logs or analytics, reviewing retention, proving isolation, and deciding whether the remaining visibility is acceptable rather than merely renamed.

Confidential inference is useful precisely because it is narrow. The useful question is not whether the word sounds reassuring, but which eyes, systems, logs, and emergency support paths it actually excludes.
