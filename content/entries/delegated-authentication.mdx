  Letting software prove it acts for you without handing it your whole face and hoping it behaves.
plainDefinition: |
  Delegated authentication is the process of allowing an agent, service, or application to access systems on behalf of a user or organisation without simply sharing the user's primary credentials.
whyExists: |
  Agents need to act across tools, but password sharing and long-lived human sessions make every action look like the person did it directly. Delegated authentication creates a separate, scoped way to say who authorised the action, what the delegate may do, and when that authority ends.
misuse: |
  It is misused when "the user logged in once" becomes permission for an assistant to roam through connected systems. Authentication proves a relationship. It does not automatically prove that every future tool call is wanted, safe, or in scope.
practicalMeaning: |
  In practice it means scoped tokens, consent screens, service identities, revocation, expiry, step-up approval, and logs that distinguish the human from the agent acting under delegated authority.

Delegation should leave fingerprints. If the system cannot tell whether a person clicked the button or an agent clicked it for them, it has not delegated authority. It has misplaced it.
