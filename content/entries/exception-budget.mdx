devilDefinition: |
  The number of rescues, overrides, and special-case interventions a workflow can survive before the economics stop pretending to be scalable.
plainDefinition: |
  Exception budget is the practical limit on how many manual recoveries, edge-case interventions, or policy exceptions an AI workflow can absorb before its cost, latency, or risk profile becomes unacceptable.
whyExists: |
  No live workflow is spotless. Some exceptions are normal. The term exists because teams need a way to ask how much awkward rescue work the operating model can afford before the automation story becomes financially or operationally dishonest.
misuse: |
  It gets mistaken for an invitation to normalise bad quality. An exception budget is not permission to accumulate endless weirdness. It is a way of quantifying how much weirdness the system can tolerate while the underlying problems are fixed or the scope is narrowed.
practicalMeaning: |
  In practice it usually means counting human rescues, escalations, manual edits, override approvals, and out-of-band clean-up work, then admitting that a workflow needing constant exceptional handling is not cheaper merely because the exceptional labour occurs off the slide.

  "The automation looked efficient until they counted the exceptions, at which point the budget was revealed to be a person with a headset and no lunch break."

Exception budgets are useful because they force a tedious but revealing question: is the system actually scalable, or is it merely delegating mess to a different ledger?
