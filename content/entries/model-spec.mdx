devilDefinition: |
  A public document describing how the model ought to behave, which is at least more honest than pretending the behaviour emerged from pure mathematics and good breeding.
plainDefinition: |
  A model spec is a provider-authored behavioural specification that describes the intended priorities, refusal patterns, boundaries, and instruction-handling rules for a model or model family.
whyExists: |
  Modern models are expected to be useful, safe, steerable, and publicly defensible at once. A specification exists so providers can state what behaviour they are trying to produce and give developers a reference for the rules sitting behind the pleasant interface.
misuse: |
  It gets treated as a guarantee, a safety proof, or a substitute for evaluation. A spec can clarify intent. It cannot ensure that every model version, product surface, or edge case will obey the text as faithfully as legal and marketing might prefer.
practicalMeaning: |
  In practice it means reading the provider's stated priorities, understanding how instruction hierarchy and refusals are supposed to work, and designing prompts, product expectations, and escalation paths around the fact that the model has house rules you did not personally write.
example: |
  "The product team called it a bug. The model spec called it a refusal. The user called it a waste of time."

Model specs matter because the era of purely generic models is over. The model arrives with prior instructions, prior politics, and prior notions of what counts as responsible behaviour.
