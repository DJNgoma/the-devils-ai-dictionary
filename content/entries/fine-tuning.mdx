plainDefinition: |
  Fine-tuning is the process of further training a pre-existing model on a narrower dataset so that its behaviour shifts toward a specific domain, style, format, or task.
whyExists: |
  General-purpose models are broad, not bespoke. Fine-tuning exists for cases where prompting alone is too unstable, too expensive, or too weak to reliably shape behaviour.
misuse: |
  It is often sold as the universal answer whenever prompts disappoint. In reality, many problems blamed on a lack of fine-tuning are retrieval problems, product design problems, or evaluation problems. Fine-tuning can help, but it can also overfit and create fresh failure modes.
practicalMeaning: |
  In practice it usually means curating a task-specific dataset, deciding how much of the base model to adapt, training on that smaller dataset, and checking whether the resulting behaviour improved enough to justify the effort and operational complexity.

Good fine-tuning is usually boring in the best way. It removes repeated prompt hacks and makes behaviour steadier. Bad fine-tuning makes a model narrower, more brittle, and harder to reason about.
