devilDefinition: |
  The rulebook for deciding when the clever system should stop being clever and give the problem to someone or something else.
plainDefinition: |
  A handoff policy defines when and how work moves between agents, tools, workflows, or humans, including what context, authority, and accountability move with it.
whyExists: |
  Agentic systems rarely complete every task in one clean stretch. They encounter uncertainty, missing permission, specialist work, safety limits, or failures that require another actor. A handoff policy prevents that transfer from becoming improvisation with logs.
misuse: |
  It gets mistaken for any mention of "human in the loop" or "escalation". A real handoff policy specifies triggers, payloads, ownership, timeouts, and what the receiving party is expected to do. A vague instruction to "ask a person" is not a policy.
practicalMeaning: |
  In practice it usually means routing rules, escalation criteria, summary formats, state transfer, approval requirements, fallback paths, and a clear answer to who owns the task after the handoff occurs.
example: |
  "The support agent had a handoff policy for billing disputes, which was fortunate because its earlier strategy was to apologise until the invoice felt seen."
